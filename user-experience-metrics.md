# How we measure user experience for the NHS App

Measuring the user experience and usability of the NHS App can be tricky because of how connected it is to a wider set of experiences that extend beyond the app itself. The end of a transaction in the app is not usually the end of a user’s experience with the service, or the point at which their user need is met.

It is important for us to measure how effective the app elements of the service are, and also try to measure how effective the app is in delivering the wider service outcome.

A set of shared metrics will help us understand our overall progress over time.

When it comes to measuring success in a user-centred way, we need to be measuring against how effective we are at meeting user needs. A [user-centred performance framework](https://dataingovernment.blog.gov.uk/2016/11/02/setting-up-a-perfo) can help.

Sometimes, what users say is different to what they do. We should always be looking to triangulate our methods and use a combination of quantitative and qualitative user research methods, analytics, behavioural data and other insights.

The following metrics are aligned to how services are generally assessed in User Experience for usability, user workload, and satisfaction.

## Service data

The GDS Service Standard sets out four mandatory KPIs that all services must measure and publish.  

- [cost per transaction](https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/measuring-success/measuring-cost-per-transaction)  
- [user satisfaction](https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/measuring-success/measuring-user-satisfaction)  
- [completion rate](https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/measuring-success/measuring-completion-rate)  
- [digital take-up](https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/measuring-success/measuring-digital-take-up)

They aren't the only KPIs we should measure but they are a good starting point. From a user experience perspective, the last three are the ones we should focus on.

They can be applied to specific services that users access through the app. They are more geared to transactional services, so they may not work for services like app messaging.

Often, there are a lot of constraints to measuring these. That doesn't mean we shouldn't try, we just need to document the constraints and work hard to try to reduce them.



## User research evaluation methods

You can use the metrics below as literal research questions to ask during a survey, an interview, or a usability testing session, or categories to track across methods.

There are specific methods around measuring for user experience which, using the metrics below, we should consider using.

### System Usability Scale (SUS)

These are adapted slightly from the general [SUS questions](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/system-usability-scale?srsltid=AfmBOoo2_gO56E8kXfjikvKtoOFlOyDxlJ3e5py_GCM0HEUFOJpQvOmB) used to make the language more applicable to our context:

- I think that I would like to use this service frequently.  
- I found the service unnecessarily complex.  
- I thought the service was easy to use.  
- I think that I would need the support of a technical person to be able to use this service.  
- I found the various functions in this service were well integrated.  
- I thought there was too much inconsistency in this service.  
- I would imagine that most people would learn to use this service very quickly.  
- I found the service very cumbersome to use.  
- I felt very confident using the service.  
- I needed to learn a lot of things before I could get going with this service.

### Single Ease Question (SEQ)

These are questions using a [Likert scale](https://www.qualtrics.com/en-gb/experience-management/research/likert-scales/). Examples:

- How satisfied or dissatisfied would you say you are with the NHS App? (1 = very difficult, 7 = very easy)  
- How easy or difficult is it to complete tasks using the NHS App? (1 = very difficult, 7 = very easy)  
- How intuitive would you say this new feature is to use? (1–7 usability scale)

For external integrated systems, providing us with data on these would support our assessment that a positive user experience is being provided.



## Metrics

Use these metrics for each principle to track and evaluate whether design work is good.

### Valuable

- "Were you able to complete the task you came here for today?"  
- "How satisfied or dissatisfied would you say you are with the NHS App?"  
- "How satisfied or dissatisfied would you say you are with the feature we have shown you today?"  
- Measure conversion rate of users starting a journey to reaching a completion state (only suitable on journeys with a consistent journey)

### Simple

- "How easy or difficult do you find it to use the NHS App?"  
- "How easy or difficult is it to complete tasks using the NHS App?"  
- "How easy or difficult did you find it to find that information?"  
- "How easy or difficult would you say it was to complete that task?" (1–7 scale for [usability metrics](https://www.uxtweak.com/usability-testing/metrics/))  
- Measure how many users are able to identify the correct place in the tree test for finding something

### Efficient

- "How efficient would you say the NHS App is to use?"  
- "How do you feel about how much information you are being shown?"  
- Measure time on task for consistent journeys with no variability  
- Measure how long it takes users to go through tasks with fixed variables

### Consistent

- "How intuitive would you say the NHS App is to use?"  
- "How intuitive would you say this new feature is to use?" (1–7 scale for [usability metrics](https://www.uxtweak.com/usability-testing/metrics/))  
- "How easy or difficult do you find it to learn how to do new tasks within the NHS App?"  
- "How easy or difficult do you find it to teach patients on how to use the NHS App?"

### Generous

- "How demanding did you find that task?"  
- "How do you feel about the NHS App filling in information for them as part of a journey?"  
- Measure via analytics how many users reach dead end screens and how often this happens

### Empowering

- "How do you feel about having the choice to use the app to complete (a certain task)?"  
- "How do you feel about using the app instead of other channels?"  
- "How do you feel about using the NHS App for the task as opposed to other methods like phone, F2F, other apps?"  
- Measure how much users are providing us with feedback via the in‑app survey and their sentiments  
- Understand how users feel their feedback is used by the NHS app?

### Credible

- "How do you feel about the information you are seeing?"  
- "How confident are you that you have completed task successfully?" (1–7 scale for [usability metrics](https://www.uxtweak.com/usability-testing/metrics/))  
- "How trustworthy do you feel the NHS App is?"  
- "How trustworthy do you feel the NHS App is compared to other channels/services/platforms?"

### Safe

- How many errors did we observe the user experiencing when using the prototype?  
- How many errors do we see users coming across from analytics?  
- How many clinical incidents are being raised related to this part of the app via the clinical incident log?  
- Are users able to understand the errors and overcome them?  
- Observe in usability testing how much users ask us questions in a session around what content means  
- Measure what issues users are getting in touch with the service desk about

### Accessible

- How do users with access needs get on with using the app?  
- How do users with different access needs rate the app on the above measures? Do we see any trends in terms of groups of users?  
- Measure how the app performs against the WCAG 2.2 standard in audits

### Inclusive

- What is the overall experience like for users from diverse backgrounds, is is markedly different?  
- How do users from different backgrounds rate the app on the above measures? Do we see any trends in terms of groups of users?



## Measures to avoid

Many metrics used within industry are not applicable to the NHS App and our context:

- **retention** – users come to the NHS App for healthcare; if they have no tasks, they may not return for weeks or months  
- **time on task** – variability in healthcare journeys makes this unreliable  
- **metrics tied to revenue** – the app has no paid elements  

We lean toward metrics measurable through user research and analytics.
